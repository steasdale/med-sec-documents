# Transcript

Fernand Braudel has described the Mediterranean islands as living “on the paths of general history.” “The events of history,” he wrote, “often lead to the islands” or rather “make use of them.”[^1] The same historian also defined slavery as “a structural feature of Mediterranean society.”[^2] Indeed, the study of Mediterranean slavery during the Middle Ages and the Renaissance also leads us to the islands. The history of slavery in medieval and early modern Sicily, Crete, Rhodes, Chios, and Sardinia, which has already been studied to some extent, reveals that slaves were part of the islands’ social systems, and that the islands often functioned as international slave emporia for redistribution to other centers of the inner sea.[^3] Cyprus, no exception to this general trend, has, until now, been neglected by scholars investigating Mediterranean slavery.

In comparison with the sources which have been used for works on slavery in other Mediterranean centers, those pertaining to Cyprus are fewer and less diversified.[^4] Nevertheless, the material which has already been published, supplemented by some unpublished sources, enables us to try and unveil the history of a phenomenon which was not so marginal in the life of the island, linking it, at the same time, to various zones of the “greater Mediterranean.” This paper’s aim is to investigate the development of slavery in Cyprus during the whole period of Latin, or “Frankish,” rule-first as an independent kingdom and later as a Venetian colony. The role of Cyprus in the Mediterranean slave trade will be treated first, to be followed by an examination of slavery in Cypriot society itself. When King Richard Lion Heart conquered Cyprus in 1191, slavery had long been established in both Muslim and Christian lands of the eastern Mediterranean. Until the eleventh century, it had been an important part of the Byzantine social system, to which the island also belonged. Subsequently slavery declined in Byzantium, but was never formally abolished.[^5] It was prominent in the nearby Muslim countries where it received a new impetus following the rise of the Mamluks in Egypt in the mid-thirteenth century.[^6] It was also present in the Latin Kingdom of Jerusalem, of which the Cypriot kingdom was, in many respects, the successor.[^7] The continuous demand for slaves nourished the network of the slave trade in the area around Cyprus, linking distant countries to the shores of the inner sea.

The geographical position of Cyprus, as well as its political and cultural heritage during the Byzantine and early Frankish period, all point to acquaintance with the institution of slavery. Nevertheless, to the best of my knowledge, we do not possess any information associating Frankish Cyprus with the Mediterranean slave trade before the late thirteenth century. This earliest reference relates to the purchase of a slave at Ragusa (Dubrovnik) in 1283 by a certain Guido, described as “vice-chancellor of the king of Cyprus.”[^8] A few years later, from 1296 onward, the acts of the Genoese notary Lamberto di Sambuceto, who was active in Famagusta between 1296 and 1307, offer a considerable amount of information on the Cypriot slave market.[^9]

The surfacing of the phenomenon at that particular moment can be either interpreted as a consequence of the fortuitous survival of this notary’s acts, reflecting a long-lasting phenomenon rooted in Cyprus’s Byzantine and early Latin history, or rather as a result of Famagusta’s ascendancy to the position of the chief Christian emporium in the eastern Mediterranean following the fall of Crusader cities in the Levant, and of the settlement of colonies of foreign traders there, particularly the Genoese. Even admitting that slavery and the slave trade were not new phenomena in late thirteenth-century Cyprus, the latter possibility seems more plausible. It seems that the political changes of the 1290s, which boosted the Cypriot economy, also influenced this particular activity. From the 1290s, Cyprus was “in the forefront of history,” with Famagusta as one of the main emporia of Mediterranean international trade and the seat of an important international mercantile community.[^10] A trip to Ragusa for the purchase of slaves would no longer have been necessary.

From among the 1,635 of Lamberto di Sambuceto’s surviving Famagustan acts, only 77 deal in one way or another with slaves—not a very impressive number in either relative or absolute terms.[^11] Yet, one should be cautious in drawing quantitative conclusions from these figures. It should firstly be noted that between the earliest and the latest of these documents there are great lacunae, two of which exceed seventeen months each.[^12] Secondly, we do not know how many other notaries were active on the same market during those years. Finally, the relatively low number of acts regarding slaves could be a consequence of mere chance, or even of the notary’s tendency to specialize in certain kinds of transactions.[^13] Indeed, among Sambuceto’s 1635 surviving Famagustan acts there are merely 44 testaments, fewer than the number of deeds concerning slaves. One cannot, therefore, draw any significant conclusion on the dimensions of this town’s slave market from the fragmentary registers of this single notary. On the other hand, however, a few testimonies indicate that during the fourteenth century, Cyprus was considered as one of the centers to which shiploads of slaves were occasionally sent.[^14] Moreover, Francesco Bolducci Pegolotti, who served at Famagusta as agent of the Florentine Bardi firm between 1325 and 1329, noted in his famous commercial guidebook that whereas all transactions registered by notaries in Cyprus had to be legalized by a royal official in order to be considered as valid in court, acts regarding testaments, dowries, slave purchase, and ship freight were not subject to this obligation.[^15] Apparently, affairs involving slaves were among the most common legal transactions, which, probably because of their relative frequency, were not dependent upon registration by a royal magistrate. The analysis of Sambuceto’s notarial deeds in conjunction with other sources reveals not only Famagusta’s role in the international slave trade, but also the growing importance of slavery in Cyprus itself during the fourteenth century, to which we shall return later.

Information on slaves gleaned from Sambuceto’s acts has been arranged in Table I in the appendix. The commercial acts dealing with slaves generally specify, beside the slave’s age and sex, his or her color and origin, which allows us to try and trace the provenance of these people. The slaves who appear in Sambuceto’s notarial deeds are of a considerable ethnic variety-a characteristic trait of the Mediterranean slave trade in the later Middle Ages.[^16] Saracen, or Arab and Turkish slaves, constitute an important group. Warfare and piracy were undoubtedly a constant source of supply, especially as far as Muslim slaves were concerned, but, as M. Balard has demonstrated, the main source of supply in the Genoese commercial network, to which Famagusta was also linked, was the slave trade.[^17] Slaves described as Turks or Armenians could have reached Cyprus via the kingdom of Lesser Armenia, the Turkish *ghazi* principalities of western Anatolia, or the towns of Satalia and Candelore in southern Anatolia, all of which were active in this trade.[^18] Tartar, Russian, Mongolian, Circassian, Magyar, and Cuman slaves were brought by Genoese or Venetian merchants from the Black Sea area.[^19] Black slaves were brought by Muslim merchants from the sub-Saharan kingdoms via north African ports. They could be encountered both in Muslim and Christian Mediterranean lands and hinterlands before the arrival of the Portuguese to the west African coast.[^20] Greek slaves also appear in the slave market of Famagusta, though not as preponderantly as a few decades later.[^21] The group of six Jews, redeemed by a Jew from Palermo, represents another typical Mediterranean phenomenon: travel always involved danger, but Jews were particularly vulnerable at sea.[^22]

One is struck by the tender age of many slaves who appear in these documents. The younger slaves were probably destined for domestic service, particularly the female ones. But in addition, there are quite a few adult slaves, bringing the average age, in those 50 cases where age is indicated (generally with a certain degree of approximation) to 18.5. Counting male and female slaves, whenever the sex is mentioned, brings us to 49 male as against 37 female slaves. These data indicate that slaves acquired at Famagusta were not merely destined for domestic purposes. We shall soon return to this issue.

Among the contracting parties in the commercial acts, the Genoese are the most prominent. Some of them originated from the towns of the Ligurian "rivieras", such as Savona, Sestri Ponente, Noli, Voltri, Chiavari, or Portovenere. This Ligurian preponderance is not merely a consequence of the notary’s origin (as a Genoese, he must have mainly served his fellow countrymen). Genoese were in fact the most active slave merchants in the eastern Mediterranean at that period.[^23] Beside the Genoese we encounter merchants from Piacenza, Pisa, Ancona, Barcelona, Saragossa, Messina, and Palermo. Famagusta’s rise as an important commercial center was entirely dependent on the presence of these foreign merchants, who often resided there for many years and even acquired local citizenship. The absence of Venetians from Sambuceto’s acts regarding slaves is most remarkable. Until 1299 their absence is nearly total, not only with regard to slaves, certainly a reflection of the war which was then raging between the two maritime communes. Yet, after that date many Venetians appear in Sambuceto’s acts, but none of them in documents regarding slaves, a fact which can hardly be attributed to mere chance. Neither can it be attributed to a moral attitude, considering that Venetians were generally most active in the Mediterranean slave trade. Moreover, we know about the presence of a Venetian commercial colony at Famagusta, headed by a consul, from 1296 at the latest.[^24] It seems that the Venetians tended, at least during those years, to concentrate their slave-trade activities in Crete, which was a major emporium of Mediterranean slave trade during the fourteenth and fifteenth century.[^25] As a Venetian colony, Crete must have offered the Venetian merchants many advantages over their competitors in this lucrative activity. However, as will be demonstrated below, the Famagustan slave market attracted Venetian merchants in later years.

Slave prices are generally indicated in Sambuceto’s acts in Cypriot white bezants, which, at this stage, were already units of account, and not real coins.[^26] In a few cases prices are mentioned in different currencies, such as the Saracene gold bezant, florins, Cretan hyperpyra, or Genoese lire. It is not always easy to explain the great diversity in prices between one slave and another. Thus, on 1 March 1301, a Turkish male slave, aged 25, was sold for 31 white bezants, whereas on 18 April of the same year, an Arab slave, aged 22, was sold for 100 bezants. The highest prices were obtained for a Saracene woman aged 22 (sold for 200 bezants), for a 12-year-old black girl (sold for 150 bezants), and for a male (north African?) slave, aged 50 (sold for 130 bezants). The lowest prices were paid for a Greek girl from Samos, aged 12 (sold for 30 bezants), a Rhodian woman, aged 20 (30 bezants), and a Turkish male slave, aged 25 (31 bezants). The slave’s physical condition and appearance, his sex, health, and mental capacities, his professional training, as well as preferences for certain ethnic groups and the play of supply and demand, must have all influenced these prices.[^27]

As has already been demonstrated for other areas of the Mediterranean, Sambuceto’s documents occasionally reveal the pervasion of the slave trade into different commercial and financial activities. Slaves are used as a substitute for money in *commenda* contracts, as a dowry, or as a security for loans.[^28] The ownership of a single slave could be divided between two persons.[^29] It is also interesting to note the shipment of twelve to fifteen slaves, male and female, from Famagusta to Egypt, only a few years after the fall of crusader Acre and in spite of papal prohibitions, officially implemented by Christian states.[^30]

The acts of a Venetian notary, Nicola de Boateriis, who was active in Famagusta between August 1360 and October 1362, reveal that the town continued to have an active slave market at that stage (see Table II in the appendix).[^31] Our former observation on the impossibility of reaching a comprehensive assessment of the dimensions of this activity on the basis of one notary’s acts are relevant in this case too. However, it is noteworthy to observe that the number of Boateriis’s acts concerning slavery is relatively much higher: 47 acts for a period of merely two years, out of a total number of 185 acts. Thus, no less than 25.4% of this notary’s deeds during that period concern slaves.

Also noteworthy is the change in the ethnical composition of the slave community. Particularly impressive is the number of Greek slaves, who clearly constitute the most numerous group. It should be stressed that whenever Greek slaves are concerned, the reference is not to indigenous Greek Cypriots, but rather to slaves brought from “Romania,” namely the geographical sphere around the Aegean Sea. As in other areas and periods, slaves were, as a rule, outsiders, exogenous to the societies which exploited them.[^32] The provenance of Greek slaves is either indicated by referring to them as “Greek,” or as *<span lang="la">Griffonus</span>*/*<span lang="la">Griffona</span>*, by mentioning their origin from “Romania,” and more specifically by indicating their places of origin, such as Aegina, Salonica, Korinthos, Negroponte (Euboia), Tinos, or Lemnos.[^33] The relative rise in the number of Greek slaves must have mainly resulted from several decades of Catalan activities in Greece and from Genoese piracy in the Aegean.[^34] Furthermore, Christians were not deterred from the idea of purchasing Greek slaves from the infidel.[^35] Far fewer slaves in this notary’s documents are described as Tartars or Bulgarians, and there is no mention at all of either Arabic, Turkish, or black slaves. The absence of Muslim slaves is difficult to explain, and could probably have been, at least in part, a consequence of changing political and military circumstances. Verlinden has noted the greater number of women slaves in the acts of this Venetian notary.[^36] Yet, our table, which is more comprehensive than Verlinden’s reveals 22 male as against 24 female slaves—certainly not a ratio proving any significant preference for either sex.

In contrast to Sambuceto’s deeds, Venetians do appear among the contracting parties in the acts of this Venetian notary, although Genoese still play an important role. It should be noted that, contrary to what has been asserted by an historian of slavery, Jews were practically non-existent among the slave merchants mentioned in the acts of the above-mentioned two notaries.[37]

In comparison with Sambuceto’s acts, those of Boateriis disclose a considerable rise in prices on the Famagustan slave market. The average price of slaves in the documents of the Genoese notary (for the 51 cases where prices were given in white bezants) is 82.5 white bezants. In the acts of the Venetian notary, half a century later, the average price is (for 24 cases in which prices are given in white bezants) 129.8 bezants. We should, of course, be cautious in drawing general conclusions from a comparison between data derived from merely two notaries, with around 50 years separating them. Nevertheless, in the absence of any indication of inflationary trends of the white bezant in terms of monetary exchange rates during this period, 38 it would probably not be too hazardous to attribute this rise of 57.3% in the average price paid for slaves at Famagusta to the demographic crisis of the mid-fourteenth century, which created a great demand for manpower in the Mediterranean world in general, and in Cyprus in particular. The same trend has been observed in the Italian and Cretan slave markets during the same period.[^39]

Famagusta was not the only slave market in Cyprus. In fact, one of Boateriis’s acts mentions a slave who had been acquired at a public auction conducted by a royal official in Nicosia.[^40] Unfortunately, no further details have reached us on this activity in the Cypriot capital, but the phenomenon might have been much wider than indicated by the extant documentation.

A considerable proportion of Sambuceto’s and Boateriis’s deeds concerning slaves are acts of manumission, or testaments involving the manumission of slaves after the owner’s death (22 out of 77 in Sambuceto’s case, 21 out of 47 in the case of Boateriis). In some cases the owner stated that he was moved to enfranchise his slave (or slaves) for the sake of his own or his relatives’ salvation (*<span lang="la">pro remedio anima mea</span>*, *<span lang="la">pro animabus parentum meorum atque mea</span>*) or for the remission of his sins (*<span lang="la">in meorum omnium remissione peccaminum</span>*).[^41] A few of the slaves included in this group were even lucky enough to receive some money or other gifts from their former master.[^42] Another group of acts includes manumissions under certain conditions, generally service for some time after enfranchisement.[^ 43]

Manumission was often simply an act of redemption, in return for a ransom, which was generally higher than the average price for slaves on the local market.[^44] In one case, in which Lucia de Ia Cava manumitted her slave Nicola de Nigroponte, the price extorted was 300 white bezants, a sum reflecting blatant exploitation of the slave’s desperate wish to return to freedom.[^45] Redemption was often made possible by the intervention of the slave’s relatives or compatriots. The documents mention six Jews, most probably from Crete, redeemed by a Jew from Palermo for the comprehensive sum of 700 white bezants;[^46] a slave from Tinos redeemed by his brother;[^47] a female slave from Negroponte ransomed by her father;[^48] and another Negropontian slave redeemed by his uncle.[^49] Two persons, described as “ambassadors” of the community and town of Negroponte (in one case the title was “ambassador to the King of Cyprus from the Duke of Naxos and the community and town of Negroponte”) were involved either in actively ransoming slaves or as witnesses to acts of redemption.[^50] Were these ambassadors sent to Cyprus in order to negotiate the ransoming of their fellow-islanders who were held there as slaves?

Considering the relatively higher prices obtained for ransomed slaves, it might have occurred that persons were actually captured or acquired as slaves with the intention of offering them later for redemption.[^51]

\*   *   *   *

It is probably not a matter of chance that no documents from the late fourteenth and the fifteenth centuries pertaining to the Famagusta slave market have been hitherto discovered. In 1373 the Genoese conquered Famagusta and held it under their sway until King James II reconquered it in 1464. During that period, Famagusta declined and lost its prominent role in Mediterranean maritime trade.[^52] Although it is possible to find occasional reference to the purchase of slaves in Cyprus in later years, by the end of the fourteenth century the island must also have ceased to function as an emporium for the Mediterranean slave trade. But this diminution in the island’s involvement in the Mediterranean slave trade may also have been connected to long-term transformations in this field. In Crete, where no such political changes took place, the slave trade seems to have Benjamin Arbel 159 declined a few decades later.[^53] In fact, the Turkish conquest of Constantinople, the subsequent conquest of Genoese and Venetian colonies in the northern Black Sea, and a growing resentment among the Latins of the enslavement of Greeks seem to have drastically reduced the slave trade carried out by Westerners in the eastern Mediterranean.[^54]

\*   *   *   *

Let us now examine the place of slavery in Cypriot society itself. From the first half of the thirteenth century, we have only legal texts on this subject. The assises of the Cour des Bourgeois, which are believed to have been composed at Acre between 1240 and 1244,[^55] include chapters on various aspects of slavery: on Muslim slaves who converted to Christianity (chapter XVI); on the ethics of slave trade (chapter XXXIV, chapter XXXV); on the inheritance rights of former owners of manumitted slaves (chapters CCIII-CCIV); on Muslims who sell themselves into slavery (chapter CCIX); on the holding of stolen slaves (chapter CCX); on the responsibility of a freedman for acts performed before his manumission (chapter CCXI); on slaves beating Christians (chapter CCXII); on slaves escaping to lands of the infidel and returning to Christian lands (chapter CCLV), et cetera.[^56] Yet, though these assises have been preserved thanks to their later use in Cyprus, they basically reflect a legal situation in the Latin Kingdom of Jerusalem. Their use for the social history of the insular kingdom is therefore problematic. There is also a problem of terminology: whereas there is no doubt that assises referring to *sclavi* deal with slaves, others, mentioning *<span lang="la">servi</span>* present some ambiguities.[^57] The only thirteenth-century legal text regarding slaves which is clearly Cypriot in origin is a royal proclamation from 1298, prohibiting commercial transactions with slaves.[^58]

It is rather significant that Cypriot laws (if we may thus call them) regarding slavery do not specify the basic legal status of slaves or the common restrictions related to servile status. Most probably, it was considered self-evident that the principles of Roman law, as defined in Justinian’s Corpus and its commentaries, guided legal practice in this field. The dependence on Roman law is reflected in the acts of manumission which declare that the freed slave regained his status of “Roman citizenship,” and which enumerate the various capacities connected with this status.[^59]

Compared with the poverty and ambiguity of sources regarding slavery in thirteenth-century Cyprus, fourteenth-century evidence on Cypriot slavery is considerably richer and clearer. Most Cypriots in the later Middle Ages were either serfs (*<span lang="gr">paroikoi</span>*/*<<span lang="gr">parici</span>*) or “free” tenants (*<span lang="gr">eleuteroi</span>*/*<span lang="gr">francomati</span>*), with a minority of several thousand free town dwellers.[^60] Nevertheless, during the fourteenth and the early fifteenth century, though not constituting one of the major social groups of the Cypriot population, slaves were nearly omnipresent both in the rural and the urban sector of Cypriot society.

The use of slave labor in the Cypriot countryside is revealed in administrative documents of Cypriot rural estates. The accounts of the village of Psimolofo (south of Nicosia) from 1316 to 1318, mention four Greek slaves (“<span lang="fr">esclaves de Romanie</span>”), who received rations in kind (including wheat, barley, and beans) and whose clothing was provided by their owner.[^61] According to a document from to the pontificate John XXII (1316-34), the bishop of Famagusta had acquired slaves in order to cultivate his lands.[^62] From the period roughly corresponding to Boateriis’s activity at Famagusta we possess the administrative papers of the estates of Gautier de Brienne (1354-56), which included three villages (Morfittes, Conodora, Dischoria) and their dependencies. Here we come across 19 slaves, 13 of whom were adults, male and female, and six children. Two of these slaves were sold in 1355 for 70 bezants each. Under the year 1356, the accounts include expenses for the transportation of four “Turks,” undoubtedly slaves, from Greece to Cyprus. Noteworthy is also the acquisition of “<span lang="oc">4 muis de semenche de langouste pour les esclaus de Conodora.</span>[^63] In the village of Cameno Prastio, in northern Cyprus, three slaves served in 1365 as herdsmen.[^64] In the 1360s, or slightly later, we also encounter slaves on the Cypriot estate of the Venetian “sugar king” Federico Corner at Episcopi.[^65] The German traveller Ludolph of Suchen, who visited Cyprus in the middle of the fourteenth century, described the vineyard of Engadi, near Paphos, where 100 Saracen slaves had previously been employed by the Templars.[^66] Diplomatic documents indicate that, at the same period, slavery was a matter of concern for the Cypriot public authorities. The ambassadors of King Peter I complained in Venice in 1361 that Venetian ships carried off slaves from Cyprus without royal licence.[^67] The extent of slave labor in the Cypriot countryside is also attested by the chronicler Makhairas, who reports that in 1373, in the course of their invasion of Cyprus, the Genoese promised liberty to slaves, serfs, murderers, and thieves who would join them. Consequently, nearly two thousand men, including Bulgarians, Greeks from “Romania” (*<span lang="gr">Romaioi</span>*), and Tartars took the castle of Paphos. The Genoese themselves landed later, taking many slaves and Cypriots into captivity.[^68]

Jacques Heers has suggested that the persistence of slavery m the Mediterranean was often a consequence of cultural and social traditions rather than of economic need.[^69] On the other hand, different scholars have recently re-emphasized the economic basis of slave labor, rejecting the long-held view that it was essentially a luxury, ineffective and unprofitable.[^70] Our knowledge of early fourteenth-century Cypriot demographic trends is rather scanty, but it is widely accepted that the island experienced a considerable economic boom at that period.[^71] Employment of slaves as household servants may be considered as a luxury, but their employment in the Cypriot countryside during the first decades of the fourteenth century, if not earlier, possibly indicates that they were also considered a profitable investment. The demographic crisis following the Black Death of 1348 and the ensuing epidemics must have increased the demand for manpower and the rentability of slave labor.[^72] In this respect, Cyprus did not differ from other Mediterranean countries, where the growing use of slave labor reflected both the need for manpower and the economic benefits derived from it.[^73] Regular supply of slaves by trade was not always sufficient, particularly after 1373, when the Genoese took hold of Famagusta. The recurrent Cypriot raids along the nearby coasts should be considered in this light too. At the beginning of the fifteenth century, the Cretan Emmanuel Piloti wrote that the king of Cyprus had captured 1,500 Saracens, who were subsequently employed on the royal sugar plantations. The king claimed that Cyprus had “<span lang="fr">grant besoing de laboreurs qui laborassent les terres pour faire sucre.</span>”[^74] It is thus in the profitable Cyprus sugar production, an agricultural and industrial activity which brought great revenues to the island, that slave labor must have been particularly remunerative during that period of scarce manpower.

With the exception of Ludolph of Suchen’s reference to 100 Muslim slaves employed in a vineyard near Paphos and Piloti’s testimony concerning thousands of slaves employed on the royal sugar plantations, the sources quoted above do not reveal any concentration of great numbers of slaves on rural estates. Although slaves pop up very frequently in the documents, their number is generally small. Nevertheless, even without constituting one of the main components of Cypriot society during the fourteenth and early fifteenth century, slaves were important enough for the Cypriots to endanger their own security in trying to acquire them by force. Christian razzias along the shores of neighboring Muslim countries, as well as the acquisition of Muslim slaves from Christian pirates, provoked the Mamluk attacks on Cyprus in the 1420s, resulting in the defeat of King Janus at Khirokitia, and in the great financial difficulties which followed these events.[^75]

Turning to the urban sector, we should start by specifying that by the fourteenth century, the great majority of Cypriots living in towns were free. Indeed, most of the slave owners mentioned in the acts of Lamberto di Sambuceto and Nicola de Boateriis were foreigners, and almost exclusively Latin. We should note, however, that not a few among these foreigners resided in Cyprus for long periods and even became local burgesses.[^76] Moreover, it is highly improbable that the Cypriots, especially Latins but not exclusively so, did not take advantage of the opportunities offered by the local slave market in order to acquire slaves for domestic purposes, or for various urban crafts and services. A few testimonies confirm this hypothesis. Thus, among the deeds of Boateriis, we find a ship carpenter (*<span lang="la">marangonus</span>*), Giulianus de Sur, who, in his testament, made on 15 October 1360, ordered the manumission of two male slaves of Greek descent probably his working assistants. The same person manumitted another Greek male slave about seven months later. [^77] In 1365, the inventory of the Latin bishop of Limassol includes, inter alia, two slaves: “Dimitri the Turk” and “Dimitri the Greek,” who seem to have served the prelate in his Nicosia mansion (*<span lang="la">ostel</span>*).[^78] Accounts of shipbuilding m the shipyard of Famagusta m 1325 mention a certain Johannes, the slave of a master carpenter from Nicosia. Two other slaves were there employed *<span lang="la">in servitio… tafforressiarum</span>* (constructing a *<span lang="la">tafforea</span>*), and another, Hasen, is described as “the king’s slave.” All of them received payment for their work.[^79] The chronicler Makhairas reports that in 1385, Perot of Montolifs black servant (most likely a slave) was executed together with his master, who had defied King James I’s claim to the throne.[^80] Among the many baptized Saracen slaves who escaped from Nicosia to the mountains during the Mamluk invasion of 1426, the same chronicler mentions George of Damat, the sugar boiler; Theotokis, the king’s builder; Nicholas, the bathman’s son; Michael, the tax collector who was a Syrian freedman; Paul, the bishop’s slave; the slave of the monastery of Makhaeras; and the slave called Stavrias, of the monastery of Megalos Stavros.[^81]

The use of Christian slaves in general, and of Greek slaves in particular, was widespread in the Christian Mediterranean. Professor Verlinden has drawn our attention to the importance of Greek slavery, especially during the fourteenth century. Greek slaves were also kept, bought and sold by Greeks, including churchmen, both in Cyprus and in Crete.[^82] This fact is echoed by the chronicler Makhairas, who considered the conquest of Famagusta by the Genoese in 1373 as God’s punishment for what he calls “the sin of slaves.” “The land of Greeks,” he writes, “was being ravaged, and the men were being brought over to the islands as slaves and captives, and our people treated them so hardheartedly, that they used to throw themselves down from the roofs and kill themselves, and some of them cast themselves into pits, and others hanged themselves, for the heavy torments which they made them endure, and because they were famished...”[^83] Verlinden has suggested that pressure by the Church and by a few monarchs, as well as the attempts of reconciliation between Latins and Greeks at the council of Florence must have reduced the enslavement of Greeks.[^84] At any rate, in 1452, we still find four slaves bearing Greek names in the Cypriot village of Mavromaria.[^85]

It is impossible, at this stage, to reach even an approximate quantitative evaluation of slaves in late medieval Cyprus. In comparison to other social groups, slaves were never very numerous in Cyprus, even during the heyday of slavery during the fourteenth and early fifteenth century. It is therefore rather doubtful whether Cyprus could be defined as “a large-scale slave society,” as suggested by one historian.[^86] In other Mediterranean and Atlantic countries where slaves are considered to have played a more important role during the same period, their demographic weight seldom reached the level of 10% of the total population. At Genoa, in 1458, the slave population (preponderantly female) has been evaluated by Gioffre at between 4 and 4.12%.[^87] At Palermo, in 1480, they have been evaluated by Verlinden at about 10%.[^88] In mid sixteenth-century Portugal—2.5 to 3% (in Lisbon about 10%),[^89] and on the island of Madeira, the first country where agricultural slave labor was extensively employed in the modern West, their number reached a maximum of 10% at the beginning of the sixteenth century.[^90] Only in exceptional cases do we encounter higher proportions.[^91] In “slave societies,” such as ancient Rome or the American colonies, the relative demographic weight of slaves has been evaluated at around 30%.[^92] The somewhat sparse data on slaves quoted above from Cypriot documents should be considered against the background of these evaluations.

\*    *    *    *

The last years of Lusignan rule and the subsequent Venetian period (1473-1571) are marked by a decline of slavery. However, although slaves are mentioned only rarely in the great mass of documentary material which has survived from that period, the occasional reference reveals the permanence of this institution on a smaller scale. Thus, in 1515, when a small village in the district of Famagusta was about to be leased, it was stated that out of fifty-four slaves which had previously been living there, there remained only twenty-nine, “mostly Muslims and children.”[^93] In 1517, the village of Mnasi is also said to have slaves.[^94] And during the war between Venice and the Ottomans in the late 1530s, slaves, probably Muslims, escaped from the island to Syria.[^95]

The scarcity of evidence on slaves in documents from the last decades of Lusignan rule and from the Venetian period reflects the continuation of the process described above with reference to the fifteenth century. The general decline of Famagusta following the establishment of direct trading links between southern Europe and the Muslim countries probably restricted the possibilities of acquiring slaves for the needs of the Cypriot economy.[^96] The conquest of Constantinople by the Turks not only disrupted the supply of slaves from the Black Sea region, but also led to the development of a new and huge slave market in the new Ottoman capital. With the establishment of the Ottomans on the other shores of the eastern Mediterranean the development of the slave markets in the big provincial cities such as Cairo, Alexandria, Damascus, and Aleppo must also have influenced the currents of this trade.[^97] The rise of Greeks to prominent positions in the kingdom, especially during the reign of King James II (1464-73), could have accelerated the disappearance of Greek slaves from the island.[^98] The delicate relations between Venice and the Ottoman Empire, which, after the conquest of Rhodes in 1522, dominated the whole eastern Mediterranean except Cyprus, caused the Republic to be very cautious in its relations with Christian corsairs, who continued to supply Muslim slaves to different Mediterranean markets.[^99] Razzias along the nearby coasts were out of the question, particularly in peacetime. Last but not least, the change in the demographic trends during the sixteenth century weakened the economic stimulus for the importation of slaves.[^100]

Yet, there was still one source of supply which could be used without hampering relations with Muslim states and without hurting the religious and national feelings of the Cypriots. The supply of black slaves through the ports of the Maghreb continued during the sixteenth century, and from the middle of the fifteenth century growing numbers of African black slaves also reached the Mediterranean from the Portuguese markets.[^101] Under Venetian domination, the Cypriot economy was no longer in great need of slave labor for agriculture and urban crafts, but black slaves, who were most probably kept for domestic purposes, could be occasionally acquired on the island. Thus, in 1530, the Venetian governor of Crete purchased on Cyprus what he believed to be a black male slave, but later discovered that he had actually acquired a pregnant woman.[^102] In 1540, during the war between Venice and the Ottomans, a shipload of 125 black slaves of both sexes had been captured by the Venetian galleys from a Turkish vessel off the Cypriot coasts, and was consequently offered for sale on the island. Six of them were bought by the governor of Paphos.[^103] However, the number of black slaves who reached Cyprus during the period of Venetian rule seems rather small. In the absence of new supply, and with the accumulating effects of manumission, death, and escape, the Cypriot slave population must have gradually been reduced to exigeous dimensions. Those among them who had originated in the nearby countries of the Levant could eventually regain their homelands. Others remained in Cyprus, transmitting certain Tartar, Russian, Circassian, and African traits to the local population.

The history of Cypriot slavery is not only interesting for itself, but also as a reflection of changes in many aspects of life on the island as well as in a much wider area which can be roughly defined as the greater Mediterranean, and sometimes even beyond. It has been noted how the political relations between the Frankish rulers of the island and the neighboring Muslim states, the relations between Latins and Greeks in the eastern Mediterranean, and even more generally, East-West relations, all had some influence on the currents of slave trade, on the possibilities of employing slaves on the island, and on the ethnic composition of the slave population.

Economically, both the rise of Cyprus as an international commercial emporium after the fall of the other Crusader states in the Levant, and later, the demographic crisis caused by the Black Death, had each its share in stimulating the slave trade and the employment of slaves on Cyprus. However, this development could not possibly have taken place without the leading role of the Western merchants, particularly the Genoese and the Venetians, with their international commercial network linking Cyprus with the remotest corners of the Black Sea, “Romania,” and other sources of supply. The change in the demographic trend during the sixteenth century, also a general Mediterranean (and European) phenomenon, influenced the gradual abandonment of slave labor. On the other hand, the opening of the Atlantic slave markets by the Portuguese had some minor repercussions even on this easternmost island of the inner sea.

Changes in the social and cultural positions of the Greeks in Cyprus, as well as changes in the attitudes of in the West towards the Orthodox Greek effected the composition of slave population on the island. The enslavement of Greeks, a phenomenon which had reached considerable proportions around the middle of the fourteenth century, due to “favorable” political circumstances in the Aegean, was later practically abandoned.

Finally, the history of Cypriot slavery allows us to observe the durability of this phenomenon. From Antiquity until modern times, in various forms and degrees, it was always present in the Mediterranean world, and probably also in Cyprus, an insular microcosmos of Mediterranean slavery. It is most unlikely that slavery had been absent from the island before the Frankish takeover of 1191, and it certainly did not disappear from the island after the Turkish conquest of 1571. Indeed, During the long Ottoman rule it continued to be, to some degree, part of the Cypriot social system right through the second half of the nineteenth century.[^ 104]
